Digital cities and the welfare state

Every day, often without us thinking about it, we participate in "smart city" activities, such as smart mobility, smart health services and smart technology. Oslo is one of the most developed smart cities in Europe (The Agility Effect, 2019) , and as students in this city, we participate in a number of smart activities. Public transportation is one activity that we all use almost every day, and rely on to travel efficiently. For example, Google maps or the “Ruter-app” will give alternatives for how to get from one place to another, whether it’s by walking, a combination of public transport options or by biking. You also get to see the time that is left before your transportation arrives on either the metro or the bus station. Another type of smart activity that we often participate in, is smart transactions. This can be for example the “Vipps-app”, self checkout in stores, payment and ordering with QR codes and through IoT devices. These payment alternatives are time saving and relatively easy to use for both us customers and for the other party. One smart activity that students especially get to experience a lot is remote teaching. This has escalated after the COVID-19 pandemic, but is probably here to stay as it allows freedom to participate in education from wherever fits the student.

The corona crisis has changed every-day life for everyone, and new innovations have helped us maintain somewhat of a normal life, despite having to keep a distance from other people. One example of this is digital health services, like video conferences with doctors and psychologists. This is something that hasn't been used in Norway before the pandemic, but that is now offered by many doctors (Direktoratet for e-helse, 2020). Helsedirektoratet (2020) points out that using video conferences as a doctor's appointment is more accessible for the patients, especially for those that live far away or don't have access to a car. Another innovation that has been taken into use on a much larger scale than before the corona crisis is using QR codes for ordering food and drinks at restaurants and bars. This minimizes the customers and servers moving about and contact between people. Besides this, it also allows for easier modifications to an order, and payment for one's order is done straight away so that the customer doesn’t have to wait for a bill when they’re done and ready to leave. This can also make it easier for larger groups to order and make payments individually, instead of having the server split the bill for them with a payment terminal. Both digital health services like video conferences and the use of QR codes are relatively new solutions that make every-day easier for most people, and they have been taken into use. Therefore, we think these qualify as innovations.
